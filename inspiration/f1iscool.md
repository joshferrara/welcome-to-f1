# A Newbie's Guide to Formula 1

Over the past few years I’ve found myself waking up early on Saturday and Sunday mornings to drink coffee and watch Formula 1. The routine has become one of my true pleasures, a source of continuous excitement, the starting point of a new hobby, and how I've bonded further with some friends.

Tons of other people are beginning their foray into Formula 1 fandom right now, like myself a few years ago. Notably, Netflix's [Drive To Survive series](https://www.netflix.com/title/80204890) (Season 3, behind the scenes of the 2020 season, is now live) gave many new fans a taste of the human side of the sport. But where the TV show focuses on the highly-dramatic, I noticed many people wanted more information about the sport itself.

So whether you’ve watched a couple races and want to learn more, saw the Netflix series, or really have zero idea what it’s all about... This guide is for you. Its goal was not to be well-written, totally accurate, or a perfect representation of Formula 1. It's just a quick intro to help you get into the sport and start knowing what to pay attention to.

Holy shit, where do we start when discussing Formula 1? People new to the sport are always surprised by some of the insane details. Let’s begin with a few of the basics…

> The cars reach top speeds of around 220+ mph

But don't worry, they're not just driving around in circles. They're driving on complex tracks with unique layouts, and occasionally on city streets like in Monaco and Singapore.

> Pit stops during races take about 2 seconds each and the teams replace all 4 tyres

All 4 off. All 4 on. 2 seconds.

But... the current pit stop world record was set by Red Bull during 2019: 1.82 seconds.

> The lap time differences between drivers is regularly within hundredths or thousandths of a second

This often comes down to minor setup differences in the cars, braking at just the right moment, taking a turn at the exact correct angle, or one of numerous other details. These are athletes performing at their peak, and one foot put wrong can mean your car is in the wall and out of the race.

[Watch an example from the 2019 season](https://www.formula1.com/en/latest/article.onboard-comparison-how-leclerc-beat-hamilton-to-singapore-pole.4QYgQYO1JN3yNXwkvFdNu.html)

> Each team designs and builds an entirely new car for every single season (2 cars actually, one for each driver)

In-part because the rules and regulations change, but mostly to match the progress of their competitors. Each little bit matters. And the cars keep developing over the season. The fastest car at the start of the year could very well be the slowest at the end if they didn't keep improving.

> Formula 1 drivers are pretty incredible athletes

Let's lay out a race weekend for a F1 driver...

They’re driving on very challenging and complex tracks (see the schedule calendar below; these ain't ovals)...

For ~90 minutes straight, with zero chance for a break...

At average speeds of ~150mph and top speeds of 220mph…

While attacking cars in front, and defending cars from behind...

Where any tiny mistake at those speeds (like pushing the gas pedal too hard or turning a little too sharply) means your car is in the wall, you’re out of the race, and your team has to spend millions of dollars to rebuild the car…

On top of keeping track of your tyre and engine temperatures, changing fuel mixture and engine settings on the steering wheel, tweaking brake bias and differential settings, hitting their braking points within inches of perfection, and more.

All while maintaining a perfectly dialed-in focus the entire time. Any minor lapse almost surely means failure.

> Sparks fly out the back of the cars when they go fast

The aerodynamic downforce is so high that it pushes the cars into the ground at the highest speeds. There are [titanium skid blocks](https://www.theatlantic.com/photo/2018/09/sparks-fly-on-f1-racetracks/571288/) on the bottom of the cars which make the sparks.

> There’s only 10 teams, 20 drivers, and ~23 races. So it’s very easy to learn and follow the sport without being overwhelmed

> F1 is a team sport. It needs everybody to win. The driver's only the closer.

The best teams produce the best technology and do it faster than the others. It’s these little and big wins which add up, year after year. Drivers are the closers. But the teams lay all the groundwork.

Formula 1 cars are known for their insane aerodynamic designs. The aerodynamics design functions in-part to help with speed, by reducing drag so the car moves more quickly through air going in a straight line. This helps with top-speed, in conjunction with the engine.

More importantly, the aerodynamics help produce downforce on the cars. Downforce is essentially the opposite of aerodynamic lift (what an airplane wing makes). It pushes the car downward so it sticks to the road better. Formula 1 cars have so much downforce they are said to be able to drive upside-down in a tunnel.

![](https://file.notion.so/f/f/ba753756-4fb9-49e4-813b-8fb38cfadd69/12e7efb5-dcaf-4875-a941-12e7c542a967/STRM3D_2DIR_optimize.gif?table=block&id=93b043d3-af6d-4dd2-92dd-d7dcdf01c0a0&spaceId=ba753756-4fb9-49e4-813b-8fb38cfadd69&expirationTimestamp=1772582400000&signature=nbPFGQ5TpIe2z3AaOsEjhZBhCP8xV3Tt8m4B8rfd6TU)

This means you’ll often see Formula 1 cars drive through a turn at a speed which you wouldn’t believe if you didn’t know it was possible. The sort of turn you might drive through leisurely in your road car at ~40 mph, they zoom through at 150+ mph. The drivers have both a lack of fear and a genuine trust in their cars. They know they can put the gas pedal to the floor and the car’s downforce-generated grip will keep them on road through the turn.

There are two championships every year. One for drivers and one for constructors. Drivers win points based on where they finish in a race, and constructors receive the combined total of points won by each of their drivers.

Whichever driver and team has the most points at the end of the season wins the championship. If at some point a driver or team is so far ahead in the points that it's mathematically impossible to beat them, then they win the championship at that race. After that, the other teams and drivers are still fighting for points. The teams are awarded prize money based on their position.

![](https://file.notion.so/f/f/ba753756-4fb9-49e4-813b-8fb38cfadd69/0bce0831-d17f-47d4-9f9e-fbabc9e5eea5/giphy.gif?table=block&id=7af0e463-fc77-4a40-9a7a-aecd6e3e69ce&spaceId=ba753756-4fb9-49e4-813b-8fb38cfadd69&expirationTimestamp=1772582400000&signature=iSqNH_Dp77c9ilRpCF8kIRGuRy4bBf138RfEujsKbY8)

Points are only awarded for 1st place to 10th place.

They're awarded as such: 25 for 1st place, 18 for 2nd, 15 for 3rd, 12 for 4th, 10 for 5th, 8 for 6th, 6 for 7th, 4 for 8th, 2 for 9th, and 1 for 10th. Positions 11 to 20 get zero.

In 2019, a new rule was to award the fastest lap in the race with a single extra point (if the driver also finished in the top 10). So the maximum a driver can now win in a single race is 26 points. Those extra points for fastest lap can add up and become meaningful during a fight for the championship title.

![](https://nonchalant-trawler-767.notion.site/image/https%3A%2F%2Fs3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com%2Fsecure.notion-static.com%2F4e36de4c-661f-445b-9292-694ace7b82b8%2FGroup.png?table=block&id=30269bb2-0cb5-469e-b1d8-1d9572a452ba&spaceId=ba753756-4fb9-49e4-813b-8fb38cfadd69&width=1730&userId=&cache=v2)

The car is a huge part of why any F1 team wins, and it’s always been the case. Whoever has the best car almost always wins the majority of the races. A great driver in a bad car will struggle, and a mediocre driver in a great car can win. It’s roughly that simple. (And yet it’s not that simple at all!)

Different tracks require different characteristics of a car and engine design. Mexico’s race is at a high altitude, so downforce is lower and the engines get less air for cooling. Italy’s race at Monza has incredibly long straights, so it’s a power circuit and the car with the fastest engine regularly comes out on top.

Each team only has 2 cars. Literally just two fully assembled cars. What happens if a driver shunts (cool British slang for “crash”) it into the wall during practice or qualifying? The mechanics on their team then have to rebuild the car and replace the broken pieces for the race, with the spare parts they brought. If the car becomes undriveable during a race? They're totally out.

![](https://file.notion.so/f/f/ba753756-4fb9-49e4-813b-8fb38cfadd69/6a26daaf-0f19-4a4d-9e0c-7ed67e60d066/giphy-5.gif?table=block&id=d7d4a9cc-54c6-425c-89c8-4495e1af3e11&spaceId=ba753756-4fb9-49e4-813b-8fb38cfadd69&expirationTimestamp=1772582400000&signature=PK6O8OfojHWogadRsKyEPj5HTjr7DGEjYr2SRbIisko)

The cars are developed and improved throughout the season. The fastest team’s car at the start of the season would most likely lose to the slowest team’s car at the end of the season if put head-to-head. The development pace moves that quickly, and that’s the value of a few tenths of a second in performance gains.

![](https://file.notion.so/f/f/ba753756-4fb9-49e4-813b-8fb38cfadd69/5a6b2a23-7a91-42eb-9566-6e692e63c886/eau-rouge.gif?table=block&id=bb79a030-792b-4cf9-b63b-bb464e68119c&spaceId=ba753756-4fb9-49e4-813b-8fb38cfadd69&expirationTimestamp=1772582400000&signature=vmwcsZVvya9LBdpMhioQxsRdRAGk6mi8OEpy-KEMUwQ)

At certain races throughout the year, teams will test out a new front wing, floor, or even some tiny aerodynamic parts someone like you or I would assume has a meaningless impact on performance. Sometimes they’ll only test during a practice session or one car, so they can get data about its performance. New parts are critical to winning throughout the season, or making up for lost ground if you were performing poorly at the start.

Those extra tenths or hundredths of a second are very important. Let’s say a race is 70 laps long, and your car gained 1/10th second on each lap because of a new part. That could save you 7 seconds by the end of the race! And many, many races have been won by less than that. Every. Little. Bit. Counts.

That touches on an important reference. Think about when you've been on the highway and started accelerating 5 miles per hour faster. Remember how easy it was for you to start passing other cars? Even though they're going 200+ mph, the idea is the same. Teams must squeeze out every little bit of performance. That's one or two critical overtakes during a race, or one position better on the grid during qualifying.

Drivers move through the pit lane at around 50 mph (there's a speed limit). They stop to almost the exact inch needed in the middle of their fervently waiting mechanics. Here's what happens:

Two people with jacks lift the car up from the front and back.

Two people on either side grab the car in the middle to stabilize it on the jacks.

Four people with wheel-guns loosen the wheel nuts.

Four people take the shitty tyres off.

Four other people put on brand-spankin’-new tyres.

The wheel-gunners tighten the new tyres into place.

The jacks drop the car back to the ground and slide out of the way.

Buh-byyyyye! 1-point-82-holy-cow-seconds.

You'll also see broken front wings replaced during races, especially after the first lap when a fair number are broken. This is the only other part which can be replaced during a pit stop.

You might be surprised, but the tyres in Formula 1 are incredibly important. Years ago, teams used to be able to work with their own tyre manufacturer. Now everybody uses the same partner: Pirelli. While technically there are 7 different tyre compounds, during a race weekend you'll primarily only need to pay attention to 3 different types: Soft (Red), Medium (Yellow), andHard (White).

![😏 Callout icon](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

Surely you've noticed I've been spelling "tire" with a y this whole time, right? Since F1 is such a British-centric sport, I've taken to spelling it "tyre". It's fun, so deal with it. ![🤷🏻‍♂️](<Base64-Image-Removed>)​

Of the total tyre compounds, 5 are "slicks" (i.e. smooth) for dry weather. These have maximum surface area which allows tyres to heat up quickest (friction with the road) and thus provide maximum grip. These 5 tyre compounds are named C1 (hardest) to C5 (softest), which are technical names. Pirelli chooses which 3 compounds will be available for each race, which then are recognized by their Soft, Medium, andHard  classifications. So at one race, the Soft tyre might be a C4 and at another the C4 could be the Medium tire.

![🌧 Callout icon](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

At every race there are alsoWet (Blue) and Intermediate (Green) tyres if there's wet weather. The full wet (blue) tyres can displace 23 gallons of water per second, per tyre, when the car is moving at ~185mph.

Why do the different tyre compounds even matter, you ask?? Well, it's both simple and complex. Softer tyres have more grip, which means they are faster for both acceleration and your ability to carry speed through a corner. But they wear down much faster. And the harder tyres last much longer, but don't have nearly as much grip and are thus much slower.

![](https://file.notion.so/f/f/ba753756-4fb9-49e4-813b-8fb38cfadd69/2c7f2c2b-4225-49eb-9f3a-ec5f5760d512/monaco.gif?table=block&id=8a80fa74-a721-47be-85fd-248fa117ce0a&spaceId=ba753756-4fb9-49e4-813b-8fb38cfadd69&expirationTimestamp=1772582400000&signature=YEwciEJOF1dlWxHOSTNjpQWtJ0RDM-P_m9fED8cPZdY)

One more important piece of information is that each driver must use at least two different tyre compounds during a race. So if you start on the Medium tyre, you must switch to the Soft or the Hard at least once, even if it's just for a single lap. That said, since none of the compounds are well-suited to lasting an entire race, you'd never actually want to do that. They have a designed drop-off rate, so the tyres often hit a cliff at some point and lose most of their grip. You'll even hear drivers on the radio complaining about their grip.

Each team's driver gets 13 tyres for each race weekend. A few months before the race, the teams each put their order in to Pirelli for how many the want of each Soft, Medium, and Hard tyre. This is all they'll have of each for the entire weekend, so choosing can be very important.

There are two important technical systems you’ll hear about when watching F1, which sound similar but are actually very different.

DRS stands for Drag Reduction System, which was introduced in 2011 to help the sport deal with issues related to overtaking. Essentially, the aerodynamics of F1 cars have become so complex that the air which comes off the back of the cars is a clusterfuck of vortices and what’s called “dirty air”.

For cars to achieve the downforce required, they need “clean air” (i.e. smooth, undisturbed air) which gives them the grip and speed required to follow other cars closely. All of this “dirty air” leads to less overtaking, which leads to boring races.

So what did F1 come up with as a solution? DRS. There are DRS detection lines and DRS zones. If one car is within 1 second of the car in front at the DRS detection line, then the driver will be able to manually activate DRS in the next DRS zone (which is always on a straight). DRS opens a flap on the tail wing, which reduces the drag on the car and allows it to go 6-7 mph faster. At the end of the DRS zone, the driver closes the flap to get back their downforce for cornering.

ERS stands for Energy Recovery System, which is the electric side of the hybrid engines that are now in Formula 1. There’s tons of technical details which are way above my head, so I’ll give you basics of how it affects racing.

Each F1 engine has an electric motor and its battery. Just like when driving a Prius, the drivers need to charge the battery. But the difference here is F1 cars charge the battery via the crankshaft of the engine (called the MGU-K) and thermal energy from the exhaust (called the MGU-H). So after an intense qualifying lap, when the battery is spent, you’ll hear the drivers change engine modes and focus on recharging their systems.

Drivers can also choose multiple modes for deploying ERS to their engine, which gives them the ability to dial it up or down depending on what they need. They can get up to 160 extra horsepower from their engine, which can help them get closer to the car in front or add extra distance to those behind.

![](https://nonchalant-trawler-767.notion.site/image/https%3A%2F%2Fs3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com%2Fsecure.notion-static.com%2Fc691eab1-67e6-4b4f-bb61-c8f093746a7d%2FGroup.png?table=block&id=a830a26b-d231-4c94-93e3-86c1ec8362a9&spaceId=ba753756-4fb9-49e4-813b-8fb38cfadd69&width=1730&userId=&cache=v2)

Personally, I really enjoy watching Formula 1's qualifying. It happens on Saturday, the day before the race, and the outcome is knowing where every driver is lined up on the grid for Sunday. But there are some tricky details here which explain why qualifying can be difficult to get right.

During each qualifying session, the driver's fastest lap time counts. Once the session is live, cars can be on the track at any time, meaning drivers are completing their laps while other drivers are on the track. On top of that, to get from the pit lane to the starting line, drivers have to go on what's called an "out lap", which is where they warm up their tyres and prepare for their "hot lap" (a full-intensity lap). After the hot lap, drivers will cool down their car and recharge their hybrid engine battery on the "in lap".

![🎉 Callout icon](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

You'll hear folks use shorthand like P1 or P5 (i.e. any number 1-20). That just stands for Position 3, which works for both qualifying positions and race finish positions.

F1 qualifying takes 1 hour, and is broken into three sessions:

#### Q1 – 18 minutes with all 20 drivers

The first session starts with all cars. Each car starts from inside their garage, where they're fueled up and prepared for the session. Once the session starts, teams decide when they want to go onto the track, ideally without much traffic around so they can get a clean lap. Their goal is to be in the top 15 lap times for the session. There's no other goal.

The slowest 5 cars in Q3 are locked into positions 16 to 20 on the grid for the race (unless other drivers have penalties which move them back, which we won't discuss here).

#### Q2 – 15 minutes with 15 drivers

Now, the top 15 cars who reached Q2 will have gone back to the garage for a few minutes, refueled, taken a quick breather, and prepped to go again. Now the goal is to be in the top 10 lap times, so you proceed to Q3. That's the only goal.

One critical detail: any driver who qualifies in the top 10 must start the race on the exact set of tyres they qualified on. This means if the race strategy calls for starting on a Medium tyre, you need to try to get into the top 10 lap times on the slower tyre. But, because of the out lap, hot lap, and in lap... your tyre now has 3 laps more wear on it than the drivers behind you.

#### Q3 – 12 minutes with 10 drivers

The final session is only 12 minutes long, since there's now only 10 drivers. This is usually the most exciting session. Most teams will go out in the first few minutes of the session and set a "safe" lap. It will definitely be fast, but the drivers won't push themselves to the limit. It's mostly about setting a time so you are guaranteed a good position.

Then during the last 2 or 3 minutes of the session, you'll start seeing every team going for an even faster lap. What happens is the track "rubbers in", because of the bits of rubber from tyre wear, and it gains grip... which equals speed. So now that drivers have their safety lap, they can really push it and try to gain that extra few tenths or hundredths. This often culminates in 2-3 drivers knocking each other down in the final seconds of the session as people come over the finish line. It's a thrill, I'll be honest. You'll be screaming at the TV soon enough, I promise.

After Q3, the top 3 drivers are interviewed moments after they get out of their cars. It's pretty exciting, and they're very high energy. Often drivers were just beat for pole position moments before, but milliseconds, and then they get a microphone shoved in their face! haha!!

![](https://nonchalant-trawler-767.notion.site/image/https%3A%2F%2Fs3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com%2Fsecure.notion-static.com%2F3ae72b8d-a2e6-451f-b088-93a49059ff07%2FGroup.png?table=block&id=03bb7bb1-6126-4e97-b0b8-4e2427d46403&spaceId=ba753756-4fb9-49e4-813b-8fb38cfadd69&width=1730&userId=&cache=v2)

It's worth noting that the driver on pole position wins a massive amount of the time, something like ~50% of races. A lot of it comes down to the first corner. But we'll get to that...

Race day starts with the cars lining up on the grid, a little while before the race. If you tune in about 30 minutes before the race to ESPN (Sky Sports in the UK) you'll see the commentators doing the "grid walk". This is where they do interviews with drivers and team principals on the grid and in the pit lane while they're preparing for the race.

![🎮 Callout icon](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

Pro-tip: One of the best things I did early on was buy the F1 videogame. Particularly, I made sure to time trials on each track before the real-life race, which helped me be familiar with each track. [Buy it here](http://www.formula1game.com/buy).

When it's time for the race to start, all team mechanics will clear the track and the drivers will set off on a "formation lap". This gives drivers a chance to charge their batteries, warm their tyres, warm their brakes, and just get the car ready for the race. At the end of the lap, they line up on the grid back in their original spots and wait for everybody to fall in line.

Then the lights count up... ![🔴](<Base64-Image-Removed>)![🔴](<Base64-Image-Removed>)![🔴](<Base64-Image-Removed>)![🔴](<Base64-Image-Removed>)![🔴](<Base64-Image-Removed>)... and once they all turn off, the race is on!! A lot happens at the very first turn. Who can get there first and win or hold the position? There's also 20 cars converging very fast at one location, so there's a lot of crashes and broken front wings at turn 1. Generally, during this era of Formula 1, the first 1-3 laps are where a lot of action happens, then the race settles down as people find their positions.

During the race, pit stop timing becomes really important. You'll learn more about how to utilize pit stops with strategy in the section below. But you can see below how the 2019 Australian Grand Prix played out in terms of tyre choice and pit stop timing for everybody.

Not sure what else to say about the races! Sometimes a lot of exciting stuff happens... lots of overtakes, clever strategies, engine failures, safety cars, etc. Anything that changes the dynamic of the race can make it fun. But sometimes they're boring, just like any sport. And at the end, you get to see more interviews with the top 3 drivers right after they exit the car. That's always fun.

#### Race weekend format:

Race: on Sunday and lasts about 1.5-2 hours (depending on if you watch the post-race interviews and podium ceremony). Start times vary by race.

Qualifying: on Saturday and lasts about 1 hour. It usually starts at the same time as the next day's race, or one hour after, so it happens during the same time of day.

3 practice sessions: two on Friday and one on Saturday before qualifying. These are recorded and shared, but usually I just read articles or watch video clips about what happened.
