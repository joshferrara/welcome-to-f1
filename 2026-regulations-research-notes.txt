Purpose: Educational guide for F1 newcomers

1. 2026 ENGINE / POWER UNIT REGULATIONS
================================================================================

OVERVIEW:
The 2026 season introduces the biggest power unit overhaul since the turbo-hybrid
era began in 2014. The goal is a near 50/50 split between internal combustion
and electrical power, up from roughly 80/20 previously.

CORE ARCHITECTURE:
- Retains the 1.6-litre turbocharged V6 internal combustion engine (ICE)
  introduced in 2014
- Total combined output: over 1,000 bhp (approximately 750 kW)
- ICE output: ~400 kW (~536 bhp)
- Electrical (MGU-K) output: 350 kW (~469 bhp)
- This represents a roughly 50/50 power split between ICE and electric

MGU-H REMOVAL:
- The Motor Generator Unit - Heat (MGU-H) has been deleted entirely
- The MGU-H previously recovered waste heat energy from exhaust gases and
  converted it to electrical energy
- Reason for removal: In 18 years of F1 hybrids, the MGU-H technology was never
  adopted by road car manufacturers. Removing it simplifies the power unit and
  makes F1 more attractive to new manufacturers
- Its removal is considered revolutionary - it lowers the barrier to entry for
  new engine suppliers

MGU-K UPGRADE:
- The Motor Generator Unit - Kinetic (MGU-K) has been massively upgraded
- Power output nearly tripled: from 120 kW to 350 kW
- The MGU-K can now supply up to 8 megajoules (MJ) of energy per lap
  (previously 4 MJ)
- Harvests energy from braking and throttle lift-off (regenerative braking)
- The battery/energy store has a maximum State of Charge (SOC) delta of 4 MJ,
  with maximum energy during charging of 9 MJ

FUEL FLOW REDUCTION:
- Maximum fuel flow to the ICE reduced from 100 kg/hour to approximately
  70-75 kg/hour
- This reduction in ICE fueling is compensated by the increased electrical power

SUSTAINABLE FUEL:
- Cars must run on 100% advanced sustainable fuel (previously a 10% bio-component
  blend)
- The fuel is an "e-fuel" made from:
  * Carbon capture (CO2 taken directly from air or industrial emissions)
  * Municipal waste
  * Non-food biomass (cannot be used for human consumption)
  * Or a mix of the above
- Must contain a minimum of 99% Advanced Sustainable Components (ASC)
- Delivers a minimum of 65% greenhouse gas savings (per EU Renewable Energy
  Directive standards)
- This is "drop-in" fuel - designed to replace fossil fuels without engine
  modifications; it could work in regular road cars
- Nothing in the fuel comes from crude oil
- Production must be powered by renewable energy sources
- Independently certified to meet strict sustainability standards
- Part of F1's commitment to becoming Net Zero carbon by 2030

OVERTAKE MODE (REPLACES DRS AS OVERTAKING AID):
- When a driver is within 1 second of the car ahead, they can deploy an extra
  0.5 MJ of electrical energy from the MGU-K
- This provides approximately 67 additional bhp
- Available at predetermined points on the track
- Proximity-based, similar to how DRS activation worked

BOOST MODE:
- Separate from Overtake Mode
- A button on the steering wheel that triggers a change in power unit settings
- Can be used by any driver at any point around the lap, regardless of proximity
  to other cars
- Can be used for both attacking AND defending
- Energy can be deployed all at once or spread across the lap
- Teams configure the power profile

COMPONENT ALLOCATION PER SEASON (2026):
- ICE: 4 allowed (3 standard + 1 bonus for the 2026 adaptation year)
- MGU-K: 4 allowed (same bonus structure)
- Turbocharger: 4 allowed
- Energy Store (battery): 4 allowed
- Control Electronics: 4 allowed
- Exhaust systems: 4 per season (down from 8 previously)
- The extra "bonus" component across the board is specific to 2026 as an
  adaptation period; from 2027, limits become more restrictive

COMPONENT PENALTY STRUCTURE:
- First transgression (exceeding allocation): 10-place grid drop per component
- Subsequent violations: 5-place grid penalty per component
- Gearbox penalties have been removed (as of 2025) due to modern gearbox
  reliability

NEW ENGINE MANUFACTURERS / POWER UNIT SUPPLIERS FOR 2026:

Five PU manufacturers supply the grid:

1. FERRARI - Supplies: Ferrari, Haas, Cadillac (GM)
2. MERCEDES - Supplies: Mercedes, McLaren, Williams, Alpine
3. HONDA - Supplies: Aston Martin (exclusive works partnership)
4. RED BULL POWERTRAINS (with Ford) - Supplies: Red Bull Racing, Racing Bulls
   - Ford's first F1 involvement since 2004
   - Ford supports Red Bull Powertrains in developing and building the PU
5. AUDI - Supplies: Audi (formerly Sauber)
   - Audi's first time as a full works F1 team
   - Took majority ownership of the Sauber squad

CADILLAC / GENERAL MOTORS:
- GM's Cadillac brand enters as the 11th team on the 2026 grid
- Initially uses Ferrari power units as a customer
- GM plans to develop its own works engine, targeted for 2028-2029

2. 2026 AERODYNAMIC REGULATIONS
================================================================================

OVERVIEW:
The 2026 aerodynamic rules represent a fundamental shift in how F1 cars generate
and manage downforce, introducing active aerodynamics and eliminating DRS.

ACTIVE AERODYNAMICS - THE BIG CHANGE:
- Movable front AND rear wing elements replace the Drag Reduction System (DRS)
- Two primary aerodynamic modes:

  Z-MODE (High Downforce):
  - Front and rear wing elements are angled to maximize downforce
  - Used during cornering for maximum grip
  - The default mode when the car needs maximum mechanical grip

  X-MODE (Low Drag):
  - Wing flap angles change on both front and rear wings to minimize drag
  - Maximizes straight-line speed
  - KEY DIFFERENCE FROM DRS: Any driver can activate X-mode on any designated
    straight, regardless of the gap to the car ahead. DRS required being within
    1 second of the car in front. X-mode is available to everyone.

- The active aero system serves dual purposes:
  1. Racing tool (speed on straights vs. grip in corners)
  2. Energy management tool - reducing drag on straights is essential for
     efficient energy use with the new power units that rely much more on
     electrical energy

GROUND EFFECT CHANGES:
- The long ground-effect tunnels under the car (used from 2022-2025) are GONE
- 2026 floor is flat across the central section - no tunnel geometry
- The diffuser at the rear is longer with a larger exit opening
- The larger diffuser compensates for some of the downforce lost from removing
  the tunnels
- This is designed to reduce the sensitivity to "dirty air" (turbulent air from
  the car ahead)

DOWNFORCE AND DRAG NUMBERS:
- Total downforce is approximately 30% LOWER than 2025-spec cars
- Total drag is approximately 55% LOWER than 2025-spec cars
- The significantly lower drag explains why active aero and energy management
  become so critical

PHYSICAL CAR DIMENSIONS (2026 vs. 2025):
- Minimum weight: 768 kg (down 32 kg from 800 kg in 2025)
  * This reverses a trend of 200 kg added to F1 cars over the last 20 years
- Maximum wheelbase: 3,400 mm (reduced 200 mm from 3,600 mm)
- Maximum width: 1,900 mm (reduced 100 mm from 2,000 mm)
- Floor width: reduced by 150 mm
- Overall: Cars are shorter, narrower, lighter, and more nimble

WING SIMPLIFICATION:
- Both front and rear wings have fewer elements (simpler design)
- Rear beam wings have been eliminated entirely
- Simpler wings are designed to produce less turbulent wake, improving racing

SAFETY ENHANCEMENTS:
- Survival cell subjected to more rigorous crash tests
- Roll hoop strengthened to handle 23% more load
- Increased requirements for front impact structure
- More stringent load tests on survival cell

3. BUDGET CAP
================================================================================

HISTORY AND EVOLUTION:

2021 - INTRODUCTION:
- F1's first-ever financial regulations/cost cap introduced
- Original target was $175 million, later reduced to $145 million
  (the reduction occurred in May 2020, partly due to COVID-19)
- Goal: Level the playing field between wealthy and smaller teams

2022:
- Cap set at approximately $140 million (adjusted for inflation)
- This is the year in which Red Bull's 2021 breach was discovered and penalized

2023:
- Cap adjusted slightly upward for inflation
- Red Bull served their penalty of reduced aero testing (10% reduction)

2024-2025:
- Continued gradual inflation adjustments
- Cap was approximately $135 million for 2025

2026:
- Cap raised significantly to $215 MILLION
- However, this is NOT a genuine spending increase - it reflects:
  * Accumulated inflation since 2021 rolled into the figure
  * Items previously excluded from the cap now being counted inside it
  * Sprint race costs now fully included (previously each sprint granted an
    extra $300,000 outside the cap)
  * Costs related to adapting to new engine and aero regulations absorbed
- Geographic cost-of-living adjustments added (e.g., Swiss-based Audi/Sauber
  can have 35-45% higher wages than UK/Italy-based teams)
- FIA included a compensation mechanism based on wage differences for teams in
  high-cost countries

WHAT IS EXCLUDED FROM THE CAP:
- Driver salaries (can be tens of millions per year for top drivers like
  Verstappen and Hamilton)
- Salaries of the three highest-paid personnel per team
- Marketing and hospitality costs (must be genuinely marketing, not disguised
  performance work)
- Power unit/engine development costs (covered under separate PU cost cap)
- Heritage/legacy car activities
- Some facility costs

WHAT IS INCLUDED:
- Car design and development
- Car construction and parts
- Team operations and logistics
- Most staff salaries (below top-3 exemption)
- Testing costs
- Sprint race costs (new for 2026)

RED BULL BUDGET CAP BREACH (2021 SEASON):
- The FIA found Red Bull guilty of a "minor" overspend of the 2021 budget cap
- Red Bull's submission was GBP 114,293,000 but they had inaccurately excluded
  and/or adjusted costs totaling GBP 5,607,000
- The actual overspend was GBP 1,864,000 (approximately $2 million)
- 13 areas were "incorrectly interpreted" by Red Bull, including staff catering
- Penalty: $7 million fine + 10% reduction in aerodynamic testing for 2023
- Settled via an "Accepted Breach Agreement" between FIA and Red Bull
- Controversy: Rival teams (notably Ferrari boss Frederic Vasseur) called the
  penalty "very light"
- The FIA acknowledged the investigation and penalty took too long
- Led to reforms: FIA committed to deciding breach penalties earlier in the year
- The breach was announced in early October 2022, penalties issued later that
  month

4. SPRINT RACE FORMAT
================================================================================

SPRINT SHOOTOUT (SPRINT QUALIFYING):
- A condensed qualifying session held on FRIDAY afternoon
- Determines the grid order for Saturday's Sprint Race
- Three phases: SQ1, SQ2, SQ3
- Session durations: 12 minutes, 10 minutes, 8 minutes respectively
  (shorter than standard qualifying's 18, 15, 12 minutes)
- Same elimination format as standard qualifying but compressed

SPRINT RACE:
- Held on SATURDAY morning (before Grand Prix qualifying)
- Distance: approximately 100 km (~62 miles)
- Duration: typically around 30 minutes, never exceeding 1 hour
- No mandatory pit stops required
- Sprint result does NOT set the grid for Sunday's Grand Prix
- Sunday's grid is set by the full qualifying session held Saturday afternoon

SPRINT RACE POINTS (Top 8 score):
- 1st: 8 points
- 2nd: 7 points
- 3rd: 6 points
- 4th: 5 points
- 5th: 4 points
- 6th: 3 points
- 7th: 2 points
- 8th: 1 point
- Points count toward both Drivers' and Constructors' Championships

SPRINT WEEKEND SCHEDULE:
  Friday:
  - Free Practice 1 (FP1) - the only practice session
  - Sprint Qualifying (SQ1, SQ2, SQ3)

  Saturday:
  - Sprint Race (morning)
  - Grand Prix Qualifying (Q1, Q2, Q3) (afternoon)

  Sunday:
  - Grand Prix Race

2026 SPRINT VENUES (6 events):
1. Chinese Grand Prix
2. Miami Grand Prix
3. Canadian Grand Prix
4. British Grand Prix (Silverstone)
5. Dutch Grand Prix (Zandvoort)
6. Singapore Grand Prix

NOTE: F1 has been considering doubling the number of sprint races in future
seasons (currently 6 out of 24 races).

5. POINTS SYSTEM
================================================================================

GRAND PRIX RACE POINTS (Top 10 score):
- 1st:  25 points
- 2nd:  18 points
- 3rd:  15 points
- 4th:  12 points
- 5th:  10 points
- 6th:   8 points
- 7th:   6 points
- 8th:   4 points
- 9th:   2 points
- 10th:  1 point
- 11th and below: 0 points

This system has been in use since 2010. The F1 Commission has discussed
potentially extending points to 11th and 12th places for 2026, but as of the
start of the season, the standard top-10 system remains.

FASTEST LAP POINT - ABOLISHED:
- From 2019 to 2024, a bonus point was awarded for setting the fastest lap of
  the race, provided the driver finished in the top 10
- This was ABOLISHED starting in 2025
- Reason: Controversy at the 2024 Singapore Grand Prix, where an RB (Racing
  Bulls) driver made a late pit stop solely to steal the fastest lap point on
  fresh tyres, despite running outside the top 10 and being ineligible to score
  the point himself. The team was suspected of doing this to benefit a sister
  team's driver by denying the point to a rival
- Maximum points per race: 25 (driver), 43 (constructor, if both drivers finish
  1st and 2nd)

SPRINT RACE POINTS (Top 8 score):
- 1st: 8, 2nd: 7, 3rd: 6, 4th: 5, 5th: 4, 6th: 3, 7th: 2, 8th: 1

MAXIMUM THEORETICAL SEASON POINTS PER DRIVER:
- 18 standard races x 25 = 450
- 6 sprint weekends x (25 + 8) = 198
- Total theoretical maximum: 648 points

INCOMPLETE/SHORTENED RACE POINTS:
If a race cannot complete full distance, reduced points are awarded based on
percentage completed:
- 2 laps to 25% distance: Only top 5 score (reduced scale)
- 25% to 50%: Top 9 score, 13 points for winner
- 50% to 75%: Top 10 score, 19 points for winner
- 75% or more: Full points awarded

6. TYRE REGULATIONS
================================================================================

TYRE SUPPLIER: Pirelli (sole supplier)

2026 COMPOUND RANGE (5 compounds, down from 6):
- C1: Hardest compound (WHITE sidewall markings, with checkered flag pattern)
- C2: Second hardest (WHITE sidewall markings, Pirelli/P Zero logos only)
- C3: Medium (YELLOW sidewall markings, with checkered flag pattern)
- C4: Medium-soft (YELLOW sidewall markings, Pirelli/P Zero logos only)
- C5: Softest compound (RED sidewall markings)

NOTE: The C6 ultra-soft compound used in 2025 has been REMOVED for 2026.
Pirelli determined there was not enough performance difference (delta) between
the C5 and C6 to justify keeping it.

AT EACH RACE WEEKEND:
- Pirelli selects THREE compounds from the C1-C5 range for each specific Grand
  Prix, based on circuit characteristics
- These are labeled simply as HARD, MEDIUM, and SOFT for that weekend
  * The hardest of the three selected = HARD (white marking during race)
  * The middle compound = MEDIUM (yellow marking during race)
  * The softest of the three = SOFT (red marking during race)
- Example: At a high-degradation circuit, Pirelli might choose C1, C2, C3.
  At a street circuit, they might choose C3, C4, C5.

TYRE ALLOCATION PER WEEKEND:
  Standard weekend: 13 sets dry slicks + 4 sets intermediates + 3 sets full wets
  Sprint weekend: 12 sets dry slicks + 5 sets intermediates + 2 sets full wets

PHYSICAL TYRE CHANGES FOR 2026:
- Wheel rim diameter remains at 18 inches (introduced in 2022)
- Front tread width: reduced by 25 mm compared to 2025
- Rear tread width: reduced by 30 mm compared to 2025
- Narrower tyres correspond to the narrower, lighter 2026 cars
- Intermediate and full wet tread patterns unchanged from 2025

PERFORMANCE PHILOSOPHY:
- Designed to have wider and more consistent performance gaps (deltas) between
  compounds
- This encourages varied race strategies (1-stop vs. 2-stop vs. 3-stop)
- Developed specifically for the new active aerodynamic systems and lighter cars

MANDATORY PIT STOP:
- Drivers must use at least two different dry tyre compounds during a dry race
- This effectively mandates at least one pit stop per race
- In wet conditions, this requirement may be waived

7. DRS - REPLACED BY ACTIVE AERO AND OVERTAKE MODE
================================================================================

DRS IS GONE:
- The Drag Reduction System (DRS), used since 2011, has been ELIMINATED for 2026
- It has been replaced by two separate systems:

REPLACEMENT #1 - ACTIVE AERODYNAMICS (X-MODE):
- Movable front and rear wing elements
- X-mode (low drag) can be activated on any designated straight by ANY driver
- No proximity requirement - unlike DRS, you do NOT need to be within 1 second
  of the car ahead
- Z-mode (high downforce) is used in corners
- Drivers manually switch between modes
- This is available to ALL cars at ALL times on straights

REPLACEMENT #2 - OVERTAKE MODE (Proximity-based, like DRS was):
- Activated when a driver is within 1 SECOND of the car ahead
- Provides an extra 0.5 MJ of electrical energy (~67 additional bhp)
- Available at predetermined points on the track
- This is the direct spiritual successor to DRS as an overtaking aid
- Only the pursuing car gets the extra energy boost

KEY DIFFERENCES FROM DRS:
1. Active aero (X-mode) is available to everyone, not just the chasing car
2. Overtake Mode provides extra POWER, not just reduced drag
3. Defending drivers can use Boost Mode (separate from Overtake Mode) to deploy
   extra energy in defense, adding a strategic dimension DRS never had
4. The system manages both racing AND energy efficiency (dual purpose)

WHY THE CHANGE:
- DRS was always intended as a temporary fix for the difficulty of following
  cars closely
- The 2026 aero regulations (less downforce, less drag, less dirty air) are
  designed to make following easier naturally
- Active aero helps manage the increased reliance on electrical energy
- Overtake Mode provides a targeted boost for actual passing attempts

8. SAFETY CAR AND VIRTUAL SAFETY CAR (VSC) RULES
================================================================================

SAFETY CAR (SC):

Supplier Change for 2026:
- Mercedes is now the SOLE supplier of Safety Car and Medical Car
- Aston Martin stepped back after sharing the role since 2021

When Deployed:
- Major incidents, debris on track, or conditions too dangerous for racing at
  full speed
- The physical Safety Car (driven by Bernd Maylander) leads the field around
  the circuit at reduced speed

Procedure:
1. "SAFETY CAR DEPLOYED" message sent to all teams
2. All cars must slow down and form a queue behind the Safety Car
3. Pit lane remains open (teams can pit for tyres/repairs)
4. No overtaking allowed while the Safety Car is out
5. Lapped cars may be instructed to unlap themselves (pass the Safety Car and
   rejoin at the back of the queue)

Unlapping Rules (Post-2021 Abu Dhabi reforms):
- The word "any" was changed to "ALL" in the regulations
- ALL lapped cars must unlap themselves (not just some - prevents a repeat
  of the controversial 2021 Abu Dhabi GP partial unlapping)
- The Safety Car returns to pits one lap AFTER the unlapping instruction
- New for 2025/2026: The Race Director can close the pit lane entrance while
  drivers are unlapping to prevent strategic exploitation

Restart:
- Once the Safety Car turns off its lights, the race leader controls the pace
- Racing resumes when the Safety Car enters the pit lane
- The lead driver can choose when to accelerate (within rules), creating
  dramatic restarts

VIRTUAL SAFETY CAR (VSC):

History:
- Introduced in 2015 following the tragic accident involving Jules Bianchi at
  the 2014 Japanese Grand Prix
- Based on the "slow zone" system used in Le Mans endurance racing

When Used:
- Situations requiring reduced speed but not severe enough for a full
  Safety Car
- Examples: minor debris, car stopped in a relatively safe position,
  recovery vehicles needed briefly

How It Works:
1. "VSC DEPLOYED" message sent to all teams; FIA light panels display "VSC"
2. All drivers must reduce speed by approximately 30%
3. An automated timing system enforces the speed reduction
4. Drivers see a VSC time delta on their steering wheels, updated every
   50 meters
5. Drivers must stay above the minimum time set by the FIA ECU at least once
   in each marshalling sector

Key Advantage Over Full Safety Car:
- The VSC maintains the gaps between cars that existed before deployment
- Cars are NOT bunched up together (unlike under a full Safety Car)
- This preserves the race order and strategic positions more fairly

Overtaking Under VSC:
- No overtaking on track during VSC
- Exception: cars entering/leaving the pit lane

Ending the VSC:
1. "VSC ENDING" message sent to all teams
2. Between 10 and 15 seconds later, FIA panels change from "VSC" to green
3. Drivers may immediately resume racing at full speed

Penalty for VSC Violations:
- 10-second stop/go penalty for running the red light at pit lane exit during
  Safety Car periods (updated guideline as of 2025)

9. PENALTY SYSTEM
================================================================================

DURING-RACE PENALTIES (in order of severity):

1. BLACK AND WHITE FLAG (Warning):
   - Shown for unsportsmanlike behavior
   - Serves as an official warning; no time penalty
   - Essentially "one more infraction and you'll be penalized"

2. 5-SECOND TIME PENALTY:
   - For minor infractions (e.g., causing a minor collision, gaining a lasting
     advantage by leaving the track, unsafe release from pit box)
   - Served at the next pit stop (car sits stationary for 5 seconds before
     mechanics can touch it)
   - If driver doesn't pit again, 5 seconds added to finishing time

3. 10-SECOND TIME PENALTY:
   - For more serious infractions or repeated offenses
   - Same serving mechanism as 5-second penalty
   - If not served in pits, added to finishing time

4. DRIVE-THROUGH PENALTY:
   - Driver must pass through the entire pit lane at pit lane speed limit
     without stopping
   - Then rejoin the race
   - Typically costs around 20-25 seconds depending on the circuit

5. 10-SECOND STOP-AND-GO PENALTY:
   - The most severe in-race penalty
   - Driver must enter the pit box, stop for 10 seconds with NO work allowed
     on the car, then rejoin
   - Typically costs 30+ seconds

6. BLACK FLAG (Disqualification):
   - Shown for dangerous driving or extreme rule violations
   - Driver must return to the pits and retire from the race immediately
   - Very rarely used

PRE-RACE / GRID PENALTIES:

Grid Drops:
- Penalties applied before a race, moving drivers down the starting order
- Common causes:
  * Exceeding power unit component allocations (see Section 1)
  * Penalties carried over from a previous race
  * Impeding another driver during qualifying
  * Unsafe driving in previous events

Engine/PU Component Penalties (2026):
- First time exceeding allocation: 10-place grid drop per component
- Subsequent times: 5-place grid drop per component
- If total penalties exceed the number of grid positions, driver starts from
  the back of the grid

Gearbox Penalties:
- REMOVED as of 2025 due to modern gearbox reliability

PENALTY POINTS SYSTEM (Super License):
- Drivers accumulate penalty points for infractions during races
- Points are tracked over a rolling 12-month period
- If a driver accumulates 12 penalty points within 12 months, they receive
  a ONE-RACE BAN
- Penalty points automatically expire 12 months after they were issued

REPRIMAND SYSTEM:
- Drivers can receive formal reprimands for infractions
- If a driver receives 5 reprimands in a single season (all for driving
  offenses), they receive a 10-place grid penalty for the next race

TIME PENALTIES POST-RACE:
- Stewards can also apply time penalties after the race has finished
- These are added to the driver's total race time and can change the final
  classification
- Common for incidents that weren't fully adjudicated during the race

TYPICAL INFRACTIONS AND THEIR PENALTIES:
- Causing a collision: 5-10 second penalty + 2-3 penalty points
- Track limits violation (repeated): 5-second penalty
- Unsafe pit release: 5-second penalty or fine to team
- Ignoring blue flags (not letting lapping car past): Drive-through or
  time penalty
- Speeding in pit lane: Drive-through penalty or fine
- False start/jump start: Drive-through or 10-second stop-and-go
- Forcing another driver off track: 5-10 second penalty

10. RACE WEEKEND FORMAT
================================================================================

2026 CALENDAR:
- 24 Grand Prix races (a record number)
- 6 Sprint weekends
- New race: Madrid, Spain Grand Prix
- 11 teams / 22 drivers (with Cadillac joining as the 11th team)

---------- STANDARD RACE WEEKEND ----------

FRIDAY:
- Free Practice 1 (FP1): 60 minutes
  * Teams test car setups, tyre behavior, and run programs
  * Rookie/reserve drivers must participate in at least 2 FP1 sessions per
    season (rule introduced 2022, enhanced 2025)
- Free Practice 2 (FP2): 60 minutes
  * Further setup work, race simulations, qualifying simulations

SATURDAY:
- Free Practice 3 (FP3): 60 minutes
  * Final practice before qualifying
  * Last chance to refine setups before parc ferme conditions begin
- Qualifying: Three phases
  * Q1: 18 minutes - all 22 cars participate; bottom 6 eliminated
    (positions 17-22)
  * 7-minute break
  * Q2: 15 minutes - 16 remaining cars; bottom 6 eliminated
    (positions 11-16)
  * 8-minute break
  * Q3: 12 minutes - top 10 shootout for pole position
    (positions 1-10)

  2026 CHANGE: With 22 cars (11 teams), 6 drivers are eliminated in Q1 and Q2
  (previously 5 with 20 cars/10 teams). Q3 still features 10 cars.

SUNDAY:
- Grand Prix Race
  * Typically 305 km distance (except Monaco at ~260 km)
  * Usually 50-70 laps depending on circuit length
  * Formation lap before race start
  * Standing start from the grid (unless wet conditions dictate rolling start)
  * Mandatory use of at least 2 different dry tyre compounds (dry races)
  * Race must be completed within a 2-hour window (or 3-hour window including
    any red flag stoppages)

---------- SPRINT RACE WEEKEND ----------

FRIDAY:
- Free Practice 1 (FP1): 60 minutes
  * The ONLY practice session of the weekend
- Sprint Qualifying: Three phases
  * SQ1: 12 minutes
  * SQ2: 10 minutes
  * SQ3: 8 minutes
  * Sets the grid for Saturday's Sprint Race

SATURDAY:
- Sprint Race: ~100 km (~30 minutes)
  * Top 8 score points (8-7-6-5-4-3-2-1)
  * No mandatory pit stops
  * Sprint result does NOT determine Sunday's grid
- Grand Prix Qualifying (Q1, Q2, Q3): Same format as standard weekend
  * Sets the grid for Sunday's Grand Prix

SUNDAY:
- Grand Prix Race: Same as standard weekend

PARC FERME:
- "Parc ferme" (closed park) conditions restrict what changes teams can make
  to the car between qualifying and the race
- Once qualifying begins, teams cannot make significant setup changes
- This prevents teams from running one car configuration in qualifying and
  switching to another for the race
- Limited changes are allowed (front wing angle adjustments within a set range,
  brake cooling, etc.)
