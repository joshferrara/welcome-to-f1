## 1. Mind-Blowing Facts for Newcomers

### Top Speed Records
- The highest speed ever recorded in an official F1 session: **378 km/h (234.9 mph)** by Valtteri Bottas during 2016 Baku qualifying.
- During the 2025 season, cars regularly exceeded **225 mph (362+ km/h)** at Monza. Fernando Alonso clocked 355 km/h.
- Max Verstappen set a record-shattering qualifying lap at the 2025 Italian Grand Prix, averaging **164.4 mph (264.7 km/h)** around the entire lap at Monza.
- For 2026, Mercedes boss Toto Wolff has predicted the new cars could reach **400 km/h (248.5 mph)** based on simulations. During 2026 pre-season testing, Charles Leclerc already clocked 328 km/h.
- For context: a commercial airplane takes off at about 150-180 mph. These cars are hitting 230+ mph with a human sitting inches off the ground.

### G-Forces (Comparison to Fighter Pilots and Astronauts)
- F1 drivers experience **up to 6-6.5 Gs laterally** when cornering, **5 Gs under braking**, and **2 Gs during acceleration**.
- Fighter jet pilots experience up to **9 Gs vertically** during maneuvers, but they wear pressurized G-suits and experience these forces in short bursts.
- F1 drivers sustain **4-6 Gs continuously for nearly 2 hours** with no G-suit, and the forces come from multiple directions (lateral, longitudinal, vertical) simultaneously.
- Astronauts during a Space Shuttle launch experienced roughly **3 Gs**. F1 drivers regularly exceed this in corners.
- The key difference: fighter pilots get G-suits and short bursts. F1 drivers get a fireproof onesie and nearly two hours of sustained punishment.

### Pit Stop World Record
- The current world record is **1.80 seconds**, set by **McLaren at the 2023 Qatar Grand Prix**.
- This beats the previous record of 1.82 seconds set by Red Bull at the 2019 Brazilian Grand Prix.
- To put this in perspective: the average human blink takes 0.3-0.4 seconds. In 1.80 seconds (about 4-5 blinks), a crew of ~20 people removes and replaces all four tires.
- In 2024, the fastest stops were 1.90 seconds (Red Bull in China, McLaren in Mexico).
- Red Bull won the DHL Fastest Pit Stop Award seven consecutive times through 2024; Ferrari took the crown in 2025.

### The "Could Drive Upside Down" Fact
- **Still theoretically true for current (2025) cars.** F1 cars generate downforce exceeding **3.5 times the car's own weight** at speed. At high enough speed (estimated ~130 mph+), the car produces enough downforce to stick to a ceiling.
- **For 2026, this becomes more complicated.** The new regulations remove the Venturi tunnels (ground-effect floors) that were a major source of downforce. Downforce is expected to be reduced by **15-30%** under the new regs. However, the 2026 cars introduce active aerodynamics (adjustable front and rear wings), so in "Corner Mode" (high downforce), they may still produce enough force to theoretically drive inverted.
- **The practical problem remains:** fuel systems, oil systems, and cooling are gravity-dependent. The engine is not designed to run upside down. So it remains a stunning theoretical fact, not a practical one.

### How Much an F1 Car Costs
- A single F1 car costs approximately **$12-15 million** to build.
- Key component costs:
  - **Power unit (engine):** ~$10.6 million each (teams are limited in how many they can use per season)
  - **Chassis (monocoque):** ~$700,000
  - **Front and rear wing pair:** ~$250,000
  - **Steering wheel:** ~$50,000-$100,000
  - **Halo safety device:** ~$17,000
- The total **team budget cap** is set at **$135 million per season** (excludes driver salaries, top executive pay, and marketing). This covers design, manufacturing, testing, and travel.
- When factoring in R&D, wind tunnel time, simulations, spare parts, crash damage, and development across a full season, some estimates put the total cost of running a competitive operation at well over **$120 million per season**.

### Weight of the Cars
- The **2025 minimum weight** (car + driver, excluding fuel) is **800 kg** (about 1,764 lbs).
- The **minimum driver weight** (including seat and gear) is **82 kg** (about 181 lbs).
- For 2026, the target minimum weight is being reduced (the cars are designed to be shorter, thinner, and lighter), though teams have expressed concern about meeting these targets.
- For comparison: a Honda Civic weighs about 1,350 kg. An F1 car weighs roughly half that, yet produces over 1,000 horsepower.

### Brake Temperatures
- F1 brakes operate at temperatures between **400-1,000 degrees Celsius** (750-1,830 degrees Fahrenheit).
- During heavy braking, they can spike above **1,000 degrees Celsius** -- that is literally glowing orange/white hot. You can sometimes see the brake discs glowing on TV during night races.
- For context: lava from a volcano is typically 700-1,200 degrees Celsius. F1 brakes reach similar temperatures multiple times per lap.
- F1 brakes are made from **carbon fiber** because no metal brake system could survive these temperatures.
- Below 400 degrees Celsius, the brakes do not work properly -- drivers must warm them up on formation laps by weaving and pumping the brakes.

### Driver Weight Management and Physical Fitness
- F1 drivers are among the fittest athletes on the planet, despite the common misconception that "they just sit and drive."
- **Heart rate during a race:** Sustained at **160-180 bpm** for nearly two hours, peaking at **over 200 bpm** at the start. This is comparable to running a marathon.
- **Resting heart rate:** Around **40 bpm** (elite endurance athlete level).
- **Weight loss per race:** Drivers lose **2-4 kg (4.4-8.8 lbs)** during a single race through sweat alone. In hot races like Singapore, losses can exceed **4 kg**.
- **Cockpit temperature:** Can reach **50-60 degrees Celsius (122-140 degrees Fahrenheit)** inside the car.
- Drivers face a unique fitness paradox: they must be incredibly strong (especially neck muscles to fight G-forces) without adding too much muscle mass, because **every extra kilogram costs lap time**. Functional strength over bulk is the goal.
- Training includes: neck-specific exercises (to withstand head + helmet weighing ~7 kg under 5+ Gs), cardiovascular endurance (running, cycling, rowing), reaction time training, and core strength.
- **Reaction times:** Elite F1 drivers can react in as fast as **100 milliseconds**, compared to the average human's ~250 milliseconds.

### Steering Wheel Cost and Complexity
- An F1 steering wheel costs **$50,000-$100,000** per unit.
- It features **20+ controls** including buttons, rotary switches, scroll wheels, and paddles.
- A typical wheel has around **25 buttons, switches, and paddles** (Mercedes cited this exact figure for their 2019 wheel, and complexity has only increased).
- Functions controlled: gear changes, engine modes, brake bias, differential settings, energy recovery (harvesting and deployment), DRS activation, pit lane speed limiter, radio, drink system, and more.
- The reason for all this complexity: the FIA banned two-way telemetry in 2003, meaning engineers can no longer remotely adjust the car. The driver became the sole person who can change settings, so the steering wheel had to become a command center.
- The display shows real-time data: lap times, tire temperatures, fuel status, battery charge, gap to other cars, and flag conditions.

### Number of Parts in an F1 Car
- A modern F1 car comprises approximately **14,500 individual components**.
- About **80-85% of the car by volume** is made from composite materials (primarily carbon fiber).
- Each car is essentially handmade. There is no production line -- these are bespoke machines.

### Other Jaw-Dropping Facts
- **Downforce:** An F1 car generates more than **2,000 kg of downforce** at top speed -- far more than the car's own weight. This is why the "upside down driving" theory exists.
- **Fuel efficiency paradox:** F1 power units achieve approximately **52% thermal efficiency**. For comparison, a normal road car engine achieves about 30-35%. F1 engines are among the most thermally efficient internal combustion engines ever built, despite making ~1,000 horsepower from just 1.6 liters.
- **Tires:** Pirelli brings approximately **1,600 tires to each race weekend** across all teams. Tires are designed to degrade intentionally (to create strategic variety), and a set might last anywhere from 10 to 40 laps depending on compound and conditions.
- **0-60 mph:** An F1 car can accelerate from 0 to 60 mph in approximately **2.6 seconds** and 0 to 100 mph in about **4.6 seconds**. But the truly mind-blowing stat is deceleration: they can brake from 200 mph to 80 mph in about **2 seconds**.
- **Each car has its own weather station** at the track to monitor micro-weather conditions.
- **Data:** An F1 car generates approximately **1.5 terabytes of data per race weekend** from 300+ sensors on the car.

## 2. Why F1 Is Exciting -- The Human Drama

### Drive to Survive's Impact on F1 Popularity
- Netflix's "Drive to Survive" (premiered March 2019) is arguably the single most transformative piece of media in modern sports marketing.
- **U.S. viewership jumped 54%** from 547,000 average viewers in 2018 to 934,000 by 2021. By 2023, it reached 1.1 million (peak of 1.2 million in 2022).
- **28% of U.S. adults** now identify as F1 fans, up from 21% pre-pandemic.
- **53% of all F1 fans** say Drive to Survive played a role in becoming a fan. Among fans under 45, that number rises to nearly **75%**.
- More than **360,000 new viewers** who had never watched F1 before started watching races after discovering the show.
- The new fans skew **younger** (46% are 34 and under vs. 16% of traditional fans), **more diverse** (23% Hispanic vs. 12%), and **more affluent** (69% earn $100K+ vs. 49%).
- ESPN's F1 rights went from **~$5 million annually** to **$75-90 million annually** after the DtS-fueled popularity boom. Apple TV then secured exclusive U.S. rights starting 2026 for close to **$150 million per year**.
- The show humanized drivers who were previously seen as faceless helmets. It turned Daniel Ricciardo into a beloved personality, made Guenther Steiner (Haas team principal) a cult figure, and gave casual viewers a reason to care about qualifying and strategy.

### Famous Rivalries

**Senna vs. Prost (1988-1993):**
- The most legendary rivalry in motorsport history. Two McLaren teammates, polar opposites in every way: Senna was the instinctive, passionate Brazilian; Prost was the calculated, intellectual Frenchman.
- The rivalry produced two of the most controversial moments in F1 history:
  - **Suzuka 1989:** Prost and Senna collided at the chicane. Prost retired. Senna rejoined, won, but was disqualified for cutting the chicane. Prost won the championship.
  - **Suzuka 1990:** Senna deliberately drove into Prost at the first corner, taking both out and clinching the title. He later admitted the move was intentional.
- Senna's tragic death at Imola in 1994 cemented this rivalry as the defining story of F1's golden era.

**Hamilton vs. Rosberg (2013-2016):**
- Childhood karting friends who became bitter enemies as Mercedes teammates.
- Together they won **54 of 78 races** over four seasons of total Mercedes dominance.
- Their rivalry destroyed a friendship: on-track collisions at Spain 2016 (taking each other out on lap 1), Austria 2016, and numerous qualifying battles made headlines.
- The team threatened to suspend both drivers at the height of tensions.
- Rosberg won the 2016 championship by 5 points after Hamilton tried tactics in the Abu Dhabi finale (driving slowly to back Rosberg into traffic).
- **The shocking ending:** Just 5 days after winning his first World Championship, Rosberg announced his retirement from F1. He walked away at the peak. It remains one of the most stunning moments in sports history.

**Hamilton vs. Verstappen (2021):**
- A season-long battle between the established GOAT contender (Hamilton, 7x champion) and the fearless young challenger (Verstappen, then 24 years old).
- Key flashpoints: Silverstone collision (Verstappen 51G crash), Monza collision (Verstappen's car literally on top of Hamilton's, saved by the Halo), Saudi Arabia (chaotic race with multiple restarts and contact), Brazil (Hamilton's legendary comeback from the back of the grid).
- Went to the final race dead level on points. (See Abu Dhabi 2021 below.)

### Iconic Team Radio Moments
These are the moments that make F1 fans erupt. Team radio is broadcast live, giving raw, unfiltered access to driver emotions at 200 mph:

- **Kimi Raikkonen: "Leave me alone, I know what I'm doing"** (2012 Abu Dhabi) -- Became a defining meme of the "Iceman" personality. He won the race.
- **Lewis Hamilton: "It's Hammer Time"** -- Engineer Peter Bonnington's iconic call telling Hamilton it is time to push. Has become synonymous with Hamilton's career.
- **Mark Webber: "Not bad for a number two driver"** (2010 British GP) -- After Red Bull moved an updated front wing from his car to teammate Vettel's, Webber won the race and delivered this legendary passive-aggressive line.
- **Charles Leclerc: "I am stupid, I am stupid"** (2019 Azerbaijan) -- After crashing in Q2 at Baku, Leclerc's emotional self-criticism went viral.
- **Fernando Alonso: "GP2 engine! GP2!"** (2015 Japan) -- Alonso comparing his underpowered Honda engine to a lower formula, expressing peak frustration during McLaren-Honda's terrible partnership.
- **Michael Schumacher: "19 qualifying laps"** -- Ross Brawn's legendary instruction to Schumacher at the 1998 Hungarian GP, where Schumacher delivered exactly that to pull off a three-stop strategy win.
- **Sebastian Vettel: "GRAZIE RAGAZZI! GRANDE MACHINA!"** -- Vettel's joyful celebrations after Ferrari wins became a beloved tradition.

### Global Nature of the Sport
- F1 races in **24 different countries** across all inhabited continents in 2026.
- The global fanbase stands at an estimated **827 million fans** -- making it the world's most popular annual sporting series, surpassing even the NBA (by 11.4%).
- Each race weekend averages approximately **70 million viewers** globally (2025 data), with peak weekends like the Belgian Grand Prix drawing **80+ million**.
- F1 is broadcast in over **188 countries and territories**.
- Races span time zones from Australia to the Americas, meaning there is a dedicated community of fans waking up at odd hours to watch live -- this ritual of early-morning race viewing is a beloved part of fan culture.
- The grid itself is international: drivers come from the Netherlands, UK, Spain, Australia, Monaco, Mexico, Japan, France, Germany, Thailand, Finland, Canada, Argentina, and more.

### The Lifestyle and Glamour Aspect
- **Monaco Grand Prix:** The crown jewel of glamour in sports. Cars race through the narrow streets of Monte Carlo, past luxury yachts, designer boutiques, and the famous Casino. Celebrities watch from private yachts and balconies. The paddock becomes a who's who of Hollywood, fashion, and business.
- Recent Monaco attendees have included: Jeff Bezos, Lauren Sanchez, Naomi Campbell, Sofia Vergara, Anya Taylor-Joy, Jared Leto, Michael Douglas, Tommy Hilfiger, and many more.
- Many F1 drivers live in Monaco (Hamilton, Verstappen, Leclerc, and others) -- it is essentially the sport's unofficial hometown.
- F1 has embraced the intersection of fashion, music, and culture. The paddock has become a destination for celebrities and influencers, particularly since the U.S. expansion (Miami, Las Vegas, Austin).
- The **Las Vegas Grand Prix** (added 2023) races down the Strip at night. It is F1's most overt embrace of spectacle and entertainment culture.
- F1 drivers are increasingly seen as lifestyle figures: Lando Norris streams video games, Charles Leclerc posts piano performances, Lewis Hamilton runs a fashion brand and has attended Met Galas.

### Attending a Race -- What to Expect and Best Beginner-Friendly GPs

**Best races for first-timers:**
- **Australian Grand Prix (Melbourne):** Laid-back, festival-style atmosphere. Great city access. Well-organized. Season opener energy. (March 6-8, 2026)
- **United States Grand Prix (Austin, Texas):** Circuit of the Americas is one of the most spectator-friendly venues. Great views from many areas, excellent fan zones, and Austin's food/music scene complements the weekend. (October 23-25, 2026)
- **Canadian Grand Prix (Montreal):** The city comes alive during race weekend. Energetic crowds, great public transport to the track, and the circuit layout produces frequent overtaking (easy for newcomers to follow the action). (May 22-24, 2026)
- **Bahrain Grand Prix:** Seamless logistics, clear signage, shuttle services. Concerts, fan zones, a karting track, and great food make it accessible even with minimal racing knowledge. (April 10-12, 2026)
- **British Grand Prix (Silverstone):** One of the most passionate and knowledgeable crowds in F1. The atmosphere is unmatched. It can be rainy and muddy, but the fans are incredible.
- **Miami Grand Prix:** Festival atmosphere, beach-club vibes, and the spectacle of the Hard Rock Stadium campus.

**What to expect at a race:**
- A Grand Prix weekend runs Friday-Sunday: Practice sessions Friday and Saturday morning, Qualifying on Saturday afternoon, Race on Sunday.
- General admission can be more affordable but you will need to arrive early for good spots. Grandstand seats guarantee a view and are worth the investment for a first visit.
- Earplugs are essential (even with the quieter V6 turbo-hybrids, they are LOUD in person, especially when 20 cars come through together).
- The atmosphere is a mix of sporting event and music festival: live music, driver appearances, merchandise shops, food vendors, and sponsor activations.
- Big screens are placed around the circuit so you can follow the race beyond your section of track.

## 4. The F1 Calendar for 2026

### Overview
- **24 races** spanning from March to December.
- Season runs from **March 6 (Melbourne, Australia)** to **December 6 (Abu Dhabi)**.
- **6 Sprint weekends** at: China, Miami, Canada, Great Britain, Netherlands, and Singapore.

### Notable New/Recent Track Additions
- **Madrid, Spain (NEW for 2026):** A brand-new street circuit through the Spanish capital. Replaces Imola on the calendar.
- **Las Vegas (added 2023):** Night race down the famous Las Vegas Strip. Spectacular, divisive among purists, but undeniably a spectacle.
- **Miami (added 2022):** Based around the Hard Rock Stadium campus. Festival atmosphere. Part of F1's U.S. expansion.
- **Qatar (returned 2023):** Lusail International Circuit. Known for extreme heat challenges.
- **Note:** With Las Vegas, Miami, and Austin, the U.S. now has **3 Grand Prix** -- unprecedented and reflective of F1's massive American growth.

### Classic/Iconic Tracks Every Fan Should Know
- **Monaco (Monte Carlo):** The most famous race in the world. Narrow streets, yachts, glamour. Incredibly difficult to overtake, so qualifying is crucial. Every driver dreams of winning here.
- **Silverstone (UK):** The home of British motorsport and the site of the very first F1 World Championship race in 1950. High-speed corners like Copse, Maggotts-Becketts-Chapel are legendary. Passionate fans create an atmosphere like no other.
- **Spa-Francorchamps (Belgium):** Contains the legendary Eau Rouge/Raidillon corner complex -- a terrifying uphill left-right-left taken at over 180 mph. Often produces dramatic wet-weather races.
- **Monza (Italy):** "The Temple of Speed." The fastest track on the calendar. The spiritual home of Ferrari (the Tifosi, Ferrari's passionate fanbase, create an incredible atmosphere). Low downforce, slipstreaming battles.
- **Suzuka (Japan):** A figure-eight layout beloved by drivers for its technical challenge. The esses section and 130R are among the most celebrated corners in motorsport. Japanese fans are famous for their incredible dedication and hospitality.
- **Interlagos (Sao Paulo, Brazil):** An anti-clockwise circuit that frequently produces dramatic races. Elevation changes, unpredictable weather, and some of the most passionate fans in the world.

## 5. Key Moments from Recent F1 History (2020-2025)

### 2020: Gasly's Monza Miracle and a Pandemic Season
- The season was delayed and compressed due to COVID-19. Some circuits hosted two back-to-back races.
- **Pierre Gasly wins at Monza (Italian GP, September 2020):** One of the greatest underdog stories in F1 history. Just one year after being demoted from Red Bull to the junior team (then AlphaTauri), Gasly won at the "Temple of Speed" after a chaotic race involving Hamilton receiving a penalty and Leclerc crashing heavily. The team celebrated wildly. It remains one of the most emotional victories in modern F1.
- Lewis Hamilton equaled Michael Schumacher's record of 7 World Championships.

### 2021: The Greatest Season in Modern F1 History
- **Hamilton vs. Verstappen** went head-to-head for the entire 22-race season. They arrived at the final race in Abu Dhabi level on points.
- **Key incidents throughout the year:**
  - **Silverstone (British GP):** Hamilton and Verstappen collided at Copse corner on lap 1. Verstappen hit the barriers at 51G (one of the hardest impacts recorded). Hamilton received a 10-second penalty, recovered, and won the race. Verstappen was hospitalized for checks. Massive controversy.
  - **Monza (Italian GP):** Another collision. Verstappen's car launched over Hamilton's, with the rear wheel coming to rest on Hamilton's head. The Halo device (the titanium bar above the cockpit, introduced in 2018 after much debate) unquestionably saved Hamilton's life.
  - **Saudi Arabia:** A chaotic, ill-tempered race with multiple restarts, red flags, and on-track contact between the two rivals.
  - **Brazil:** Hamilton took a new engine (incurring a grid penalty), started from the back, and carved through the field in one of the great recovery drives. He won from P10 on the grid.
- **Abu Dhabi 2021 -- The Most Controversial Finish in F1 History:**
  - Hamilton led the race comfortably in the final laps, seemingly on his way to a record-breaking 8th World Championship.
  - A late safety car (caused by Nicholas Latifi's crash) bunched the field.
  - Race Director Michael Masi made the unprecedented decision to allow only the lapped cars between Hamilton and Verstappen to unlap themselves (not all lapped cars, as regulations seemed to require), then restarted the race for a single final lap.
  - Verstappen, on fresh soft tires (he had pitted during the safety car, Hamilton had not), overtook Hamilton on the final lap to win the championship.
  - The decision was immediately and massively controversial. Hamilton and Mercedes protested. The FIA later acknowledged the rules were not applied correctly. Masi was removed from the Race Director role.
  - This moment divided the fanbase, generated worldwide headlines beyond motorsport, and remains the most debated moment in F1 history. It is the "Hand of God" of Formula 1.

### 2022: New Regulations, Ferrari's False Dawn, Verstappen Dominance Begins
- Major regulation changes introduced ground-effect aerodynamics, designed to allow cars to follow each other more closely and improve racing.
- Ferrari's Charles Leclerc won 2 of the first 3 races and led the championship early, raising hopes of a Ferrari resurgence.
- But Ferrari's season fell apart due to strategic errors (infamous pit wall mistakes), mechanical reliability failures, and driver errors. It became a meme: "Ferrari strategists" entered the F1 lexicon as shorthand for inexplicable decisions.
- Verstappen won **15 of 22 races** and clinched the title with 4 races remaining.

### 2023: Verstappen's Record-Breaking Dominance
- Verstappen won **19 of 22 races** -- the most dominant season by any driver in F1 history.
- Red Bull won 21 of 22 races as a team (only Ferrari's Carlos Sainz won in Singapore when Verstappen had a rare off weekend).
- While Verstappen's brilliance was undeniable, the lack of competition worried many about the sport's appeal.

### 2024: The Competitive Balance Returns
- Red Bull's dominance crumbled mid-season. Verstappen won 5 of the first 7 races but then the field caught up.
- **McLaren's resurgence:** Lando Norris and Oscar Piastri became genuine contenders. McLaren won its first Constructors' Championship in **26 years**.
- **Lewis Hamilton's emotional Silverstone win:** Hamilton won the British Grand Prix in what many saw as a fairytale moment -- his first win in over two years, achieved at his home race. He was visibly emotional on the podium, shedding tears.
- **Charles Leclerc wins at Monaco:** Leclerc, who grew up in Monaco and had suffered heartbreak there before, finally won his home Grand Prix. A fairy tale for the Ferrari fans.
- **The bombshell:** In early 2024, Hamilton announced he would leave Mercedes to join **Ferrari** for 2025. This was one of the biggest driver moves in F1 history -- the sport's most successful driver joining its most iconic team.
- **Adrian Newey's departure:** The most successful car designer in F1 history left Red Bull and announced he would join **Aston Martin**. His departure was seen as a seismic shift in the competitive landscape.

### 2025: Three-Way Title Fight and Norris's Coronation
- For the first time in 15 years, **three drivers arrived at the final race with a realistic chance** of winning the championship: Lando Norris, Max Verstappen, and Oscar Piastri.
- **Lando Norris won his maiden World Championship** at the Abu Dhabi Grand Prix, becoming the sport's 35th World Champion.
- Hamilton's first season at Ferrari was largely disappointing, with the 7-time champion often outpaced by teammate Leclerc.
- **Nico Hulkenberg finally stood on an F1 podium** after 239 race starts -- one of the longest waits in history.
- **McLaren celebrated their 200th Grand Prix victory**, becoming only the second team to reach that milestone.
- The season delivered standout races in Australia (mixed conditions chaos), Great Britain (historic podium drama), Netherlands (championship-altering retirement), Mexico (Norris at his peak), and Brazil (a race that combined all elements of drama).

## 6. The 2026 Season -- What Makes It Special

### A New Era
- 2026 represents the **biggest regulation overhaul in F1 history**, affecting both chassis and power units simultaneously.
- **Active Aerodynamics:** Front and rear wings can switch between "Corner Mode" (high downforce for grip) and "Straight Mode" (low drag for speed). This replaces DRS.
- **New Power Units:** 50% of power from the battery, 50% from the internal combustion engine. Engines run on **advanced sustainable fuels**. The MGU-H (a complex and controversial hybrid component) has been removed to reduce costs and encourage new manufacturers.
- **Cars are shorter, thinner, lighter, and nimbler** compared to the 2022-2025 generation.

### Cadillac -- The 11th Team
- **Cadillac F1** (backed by General Motors/TWG Motorsports) joins the grid as the **first new team since 2016**.
- Drivers: **Valtteri Bottas** (experienced former Mercedes driver and Hamilton teammate) and **Sergio Perez** (former Red Bull driver).
- Using Ferrari power units initially, with plans for a GM-developed in-house power unit from **2029**.
- This means **22 cars on the grid** instead of 20 -- more racing, more storylines, more action.

### Apple TV's First Season
- The first season under Apple's exclusive U.S. broadcasting deal brings cutting-edge production values, 4K Dolby Vision, and innovative Multiview features.

### Key Storylines Entering 2026
- Can Norris defend his title in completely new cars?
- Will the regulation reset allow a smaller team to challenge?
- How will Hamilton and Ferrari adapt to the new rules in his second (and possibly final) years?
- Adrian Newey's first car with Aston Martin -- will his genius translate immediately?
- Cadillac's debut -- can the 11th team be competitive?
- How will active aero change racing and overtaking?
